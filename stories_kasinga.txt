Elias had a rule about the river: never promise a show. He had learned this lesson years ago, the day he promised a group of rowdy vacationers that they would see a crocodile leap. For three hours, the river remained as still as a painting. He had spent the remaining time sweating and apologizing.

Ever since, he just pointed. If the animals showed up, it was a miracle. If they didn't, it was just the river being the river. And he found that the tourists who accepted the river as it was—lazy, unpredictable, and entirely uninterested in them—were the only ones who ever really saw anything worth seeing.

4: One afternoon, a young girl with a tattered sketchbook sat by the water's edge, far from the crowd. She wasn't looking for a show. She was watching the way the ripples caught the amber light of the setting sun. Elias watched her, curious. Suddenly, a snout broke the surface, then another. A family of hippos drifted into the golden glow, silent as ghosts. They didn't splash or roar; they simply existed, sharing the quiet of the evening with the girl who hadn't asked for anything at all. Elias smiled, realizing the river always spoke loudest to those who knew how to listen.
