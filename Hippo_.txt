Bulky Had His Own Schedule

By the time the first safari truck rattled into Kasinga Bend, the morning was already warm enough to make the river steam in thin silver ribbons. Birds called from the reeds, insects whined above the water, and the sun pressed down on everything with the confidence of a bully who knew nobody could push back.

On the far side of the bend, just beyond a patch of green water grass, a great gray shape floated half-submerged in the shallows.

"Is that him?" asked a tourist in a beige hat, already standing on the seat for a better look.

The guide did not answer immediately. He set down his tin mug of tea, narrowed one eye at the river, and nodded with the solemnity of a man identifying royalty.

"That," he said, "is Bulky."

The tourists leaned forward all at once. Cameras clicked. Binoculars snapped open. One woman whispered "Finally" as though she had been trying to arrange a meeting with an unpredictable celebrity for years.

Bulky did not move.

Only the top of his back showed above the water, smooth and gray and enormous, like a boulder that had become tired of being dry. A small bird perched on him without permission. Somewhere near his left ear, a dragonfly hovered, changed its mind, and flew away.

"Why do they call him Bulky?" asked the man in the beige hat.

The guide looked at him for a long second.

"Because Tiny was already taken," he said.

That earned the first laugh of the morning, but Bulky offered no reaction. He remained as he was: still, round, and profoundly unconcerned.

The guide, whose name was Elias and whose patience had been polished into something nearly holy by thirty years of river work, clasped his hands behind his back.

"Now," he said, "you should understand something important. Many people come here believing the animals perform for them. They think a lion roars on command, an elephant poses politely, and a hippo rises when called like a dog in a yard."

"And he doesn’t?" asked the woman with the largest camera.

Elias smiled.

"Madam, Bulky does not even respond to his own name if he feels the matter can wait."

This was not what the tourists wanted to hear. They had paid for stories, photographs, sudden splashes, open jaws, dramatic warnings from the guide, and perhaps one near miss they could retell safely over dinner. What they had received so far was a warm truck, a stiff breeze carrying the smell of mud, and a sleeping lump with opinions about effort.

Still, hope is stubborn in people who have expensive cameras. They waited.

At first they waited quietly, as civilized visitors to the wild are expected to do. They raised binoculars, lowered them, and murmured observations.

"I think I saw an ear."

"No, that was a ripple."

"Maybe he’s shy."

Elias barked a small laugh into his fist.

Twenty minutes passed. The bird on Bulky’s back left. Another bird arrived. Somewhere upriver, something splashed loudly enough to wake the imagination of everyone in the truck but not, apparently, the imagination of the hippo.

The man in the beige hat tried diplomacy.

"Bulky?" he called gently.

No response.

He cleared his throat and tried again with more confidence, as if the first attempt had suffered from a lack of authority.

"Bulky!"

Nothing.

The woman with the camera leaned forward.

"Maybe he doesn’t speak English."

"Hippos speak only hippo," said Elias. "And even then, Bulky answers selectively."

This seemed reasonable enough, but it did not discourage further efforts. A younger tourist, who had spent the entire drive asking whether zebras were black with white stripes or white with black stripes, cupped his hands around his mouth.

"Mr. Bulky!" he shouted toward the water. "Sir!"

The river, receiving this information, continued being a river.

Elias took off his hat and fanned himself slowly.

"You are all making a mistake," he said.

"What mistake?" asked the woman.

"You assume he is not responding."

The group looked at him.

"Then what is he doing?" asked the man in beige.

Elias returned his hat to his head.

"Ignoring you with great concentration."

That line quieted them for a while, partly because it was funny and partly because everyone recognized the truth of it. There was something unmistakably deliberate about Bulky’s stillness now. He no longer looked asleep. He looked committed.

An hour into the wait, the bend had become a theater of tiny inconveniences. The tea in Elias's mug had gone cold. The tourists had run through admiration, curiosity, mild offense, and strategic optimism. Someone opened a packet of biscuits and then, under Elias's stare, guiltily closed it again. The air smelled of damp soil and hot grass.

At last the oldest tourist in the group, a retired headmistress from Liverpool who had so far said very little, adjusted her scarf and spoke in a voice sharpened by decades of commanding attention.

"Bulky," she said, crisp as a school bell, "this is getting ridiculous."

Every head turned.

There was a pause.

Then, from the water, came a sound.

It was not a dramatic eruption. It was not the explosive rise of a beast startled into action. It was one long, annoyed exhale, bubbling up from the river as if the water itself had finally lost patience.

Bulky began to rise.

He did it slowly, with the unhurried grandeur of something that knows the world must make time for it. First came the nostrils, slick and dark. Then the eyes, opening with ancient, offended calm. Then the broad head itself, streaming river water in bright sheets. Mud glistened on his skin. His tiny ears twitched once, twice, like afterthoughts.

The tourists forgot themselves completely.

Someone gasped.

Someone else whispered, "Oh, magnificent."

The man in beige nearly dropped his binoculars into the truck.

Bulky lifted his head a little higher and looked directly at them.

It was not the vacant look of an animal startled from a nap. It was the steady, measurable stare of a creature who had heard every word for the past hour and had been weighing whether any of it deserved a reply.

The retired headmistress folded her arms.

"Well?" she said.

Bulky opened his mouth.

No photograph anyone took that day would fully capture the scale of it. The jaw yawned wide enough to seem impossible, pink and cavernous and lined with blunt force masquerading as teeth. It was a display so vast and theatrical that half the truck recoiled and the other half forgot to breathe.

Then came the sound.

It rolled out over the river in a deep, resonant blast that seemed less like a noise and more like a verdict. Birds burst from the reeds. Ripples raced to the bank. Even Elias nodded once, respectfully, as though Bulky had chosen to issue a formal statement.

The statement, if translated honestly, was this:

I heard you the first time. I simply did not care.

Bulky closed his mouth.

For one perfect moment, nothing moved. The tourists stared. Bulky stared back. The river held its breath around him.

Then, with astonishing delicacy for such a giant creature, he flicked one ear, turned half an inch away from the truck, and lowered himself back into the water.

He vanished by degrees. Eyes, ears, nostrils, back. Soon there was only the shifting surface of the bend and a widening circle of ripples moving through the reeds like laughter that had not yet decided to become sound.

The silence inside the truck lasted three full seconds.

Then everyone began talking at once.

"Did you see that?"

"He looked straight at us!"

"I told you he was awake!"

"That was personal," said the younger tourist, still pale.

The retired headmistress sat down with the quiet dignity of someone accustomed to winning arguments in unusual settings.

"Charming fellow," she said.

Elias picked up his mug, discovered again that the tea was cold, and drank it anyway.

"You were lucky," he told them.

"Lucky?" said the woman with the camera. "He ignored us for an hour."

"Exactly," Elias replied. "Most people only see Bulky being a hippo. Today, you saw Bulky being Bulky."

He started the engine, and the truck gave a protesting cough before settling into a rumble. As they pulled away from Kasinga Bend, the tourists kept looking back at the water, half expecting another appearance, another declaration, another glimpse of that monumental, stubborn head.

But Bulky had finished with them.

The sun climbed higher. The river flashed in broken gold. Somewhere beneath the murky surface, in cool green silence, the undisputed king of delayed replies resumed the important business of doing absolutely nothing until it suited him otherwise.
